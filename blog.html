I Did Everything Right and It Still Didn't Work

Nobody tells you that you can follow every piece of advice, do everything they say, build the thing they told you to build — and still hit a wall. Not because you did it wrong. Because the system isn't designed for you to win.

I have a website. I have a catalog of over 100 designs. I have an email list setup, a Pinterest account, an Instagram account, an eBay shop, a Ko-fi page. I've done the SEO. I've uploaded consistently. I've made the blenders and the coordinates and the trending designs. I've researched the platforms, watched the videos, read the posts from people who swear they cracked the code.

And I'm still here, watching my posts get 100 views, my designs disappear from search results, and my ACEOs sit with one view for seven days.

Here's what I've learned that nobody in the creative business space will say out loud: the platforms are not neutral. They are businesses. They promote what already performs. They reward the sellers who already have sales history, the creators who already have followers, the designs that already have favorites. If you're starting from zero, you are not competing on a level playing field. You are competing against people who got there first and a machine that has decided to keep showing them.

That's not a mindset problem. That's not a strategy problem. That's just the truth of how these systems work in 2026.

I'm not saying this to be a downer. I'm saying it because I spent months believing I was doing something wrong, tweaking my titles and my tags and my hooks and my posting schedule, when the actual problem was that the algorithm had already decided I wasn't worth showing. And no amount of "better content" was going to override that decision.

What I'm doing instead — building things that don't require a platform to choose to show me. A blog that Google indexes. A KDP book that lives on Amazon search permanently. eBay listings that build seller history one sale at a time. A website that exists whether Instagram decides to boost me or not.

It's slower. It's less satisfying than going viral. But it's mine. And at this point, ownership is the only thing that feels like real progress.

I'm still here. Still making things. Still figuring it out.

They Already Decided Who You Were — On Being an Underestimated Artist in a World That Rewards Performance

If you're an independent artist who has done everything right and still can't get traction — can't get seen, can't get sales, can't get the algorithm to show your work to anyone — this is for you. Not because I have the solution. Because I'm in it too.

If you're an independent artist who has done everything right and still can't get traction — can't get seen, can't get sales, can't get the algorithm to show your work to anyone — this is for you. Not because I have the solution. Because I'm in it too.

I was never the lead. Never the soloist. Never the manager, despite the degree that said I could be.

I was the townsperson. The background. The pity role.

Not because I couldn't do it. Because they had already decided I couldn't before I ever had the chance to try. And when you only get one chance — because that's all they'll give you — you know it. You feel the weight of it. You're so aware that this is your only shot that you can't be comfortable, can't be loose, can't be the version of yourself that actually shines. And when you don't perform the way someone perfectly relaxed would perform, they nod to each other. See, they say. We were right.

Someone else does less and gets praised for it. Gets invited back. Gets more chances. Because they were already seen differently. Already embraced. The standard was never the same.

I spent years letting my art speak without my name on it. That was deliberate. If the work didn't land, I didn't have to claim it. No one could connect the failure to me. My art is an extension of who I am, but I had become so ashamed of who I am that I needed to separate them. I needed something to exist in the world without my image tarnishing it.

Now they tell us the artist has to be attached to the art to succeed. You have to show your face. Build a personal brand. Be an influencer, a brand ambassador, a performer. And I've been told — more than once, by more than one person — that I don't meet the standard for that either.

But here's where I land: hiding is letting them win.

They want me to accept less. They want me to disappear. Every system I've bumped up against — every platform, every algorithm, every room where someone looked through me instead of at me — has been designed, consciously or not, to make people like me give up.

I'm not giving up.

Not because I have some extraordinary gift or some untouchable confidence. But because I know you're out there. The caregiver who gives everything and receives nothing. The one who gets steamrolled and still shows up because survival doesn't give you the option to stop. The person everyone calls shy when really you're just not performing on cue for people who haven't earned it. The one who was never cast as the lead, never chosen first, never praised the way someone with less did more — and you've been carrying that quietly for years.

I'm making handmade art for you — as an independent artist who refuses to disappear. Art that doesn't ask you to be anything other than what you already are. Gifts that say I see you, even when the world keeps looking past you.

There's only one of you. And that's not a consolation prize. That's the whole point.

You weren't too much. You were just in the wrong rooms, with the wrong people, who either couldn't see your light or saw it and wanted to put it out.

Find your rooms. I'll be in them too.
